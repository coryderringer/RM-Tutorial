To expand on this point, and explore some of the advantages of within subjects designs, let's look at one set of hypothetical data presented as a between-subjects comparison and as a within-subjects comparison (each graph displays the same dataset), with participants' weights as our dependent variable.

Remember: in a between-subjects design, we want to know if the group averages are different after some treatment. In a within-subjects design, we want to know if one treatment consistently works better than the other for most people.

Intuitively, which chart would you say shows the largest difference between the two conditions?




This example highlights another advantage of within-subject designs: they're insensitive to individual differences. Look at the wide distribution of weights in each condition for both graphs. When we want to know if the difference between the group means is reliably (i.e. significantly) greater than zero, we have to have a context against which to judge the difference. That context is the variation in each group: the wider each group is spread, the harder it is for our statistical test to tell if there is a real difference between the group means.

In a within-subjects design, the wide distribution for each condition wouldn't actually matter, because the lines that connect them are generally sloping in the same direction.

Here we can see that the between-subjects design does much better when individual differences are small (p = .026 on the bottom left, compared to .215 on the top left). With each group more tightly distributed around its mean, it’s easier to tell that there is a real difference between the group means. It even comes out to be statistically significant (p < .05).

However, notice that the p value for the within-subjects design is exactly the same regardless of individual differences, because within-subjects designs are not affected by individual differences.

Take a minute or two to play around with this simulator a bit. See what happens when you generate different datasets using the "Resample" button: how often is the within-subjects design significant when the between-subjects design isn’t? Is that pattern ever reversed?

    Key Ideas:
  • Between-Subjects Designs assign each person to one condition.

  • Within-Subjects Designs assign each person to every condition.

  • Within-subjects designs eliminate individual differences as a factor that could influence the dependent variable

  • Between-subjects designs examine group means

  • Within-subjects designs examine difference scores
